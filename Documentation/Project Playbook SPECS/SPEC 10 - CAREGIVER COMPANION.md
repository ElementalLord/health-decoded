Purpose
The Caregiver Companion exists because a diabetes diagnosis rarely affects just one person.
Partners begin grocery shopping differently.
Parents become worried.
Adult children start asking questions.
Friends become afraid of saying the wrong thing.
Everyone wants to help.
Very few know how.
The purpose of the Caregiver Companion is to teach support, not medicine.
It helps caregivers understand what the patient is learning, what emotions they may be experiencing, and how small actions can make daily life easier.
If the caregiver finishes each section thinking,
"Now I know how to help."
then the experience has succeeded.

Product Philosophy
The caregiver is not another patient.
They do not need the entire diabetes curriculum.
They need context.
Every page answers one question:
"What does my loved one need from me right now?"
Sometimes the answer is practical.
Sometimes it is emotional.
Sometimes the answer is simply:
"Nothing. Just be there."
The Caregiver Companion should never encourage caregivers to become the patient's doctor.
Its purpose is partnership.
Not supervision.

Understanding Their Journey
This section explains what the patient is probably experiencing during the current stage of diagnosis.
Example:
Week One
Your loved one may be feeling:
• Overwhelmed
• Confused
• Guilty
• Afraid of making mistakes
This does not mean they are struggling.
These emotions are extremely common after diagnosis.
Below that:
Right now,

they probably need reassurance
more than advice.
This section changes as the patient's journey progresses.
The caregiver learns alongside them.

How You Can Help
This is the most practical section.
Instead of generic advice,
every page suggests a few realistic actions.
Example
Today you can help by:
• Asking how their appointment went.
• Learning one new thing together.
• Going for a short walk together if they want company.
• Planning dinner together instead of deciding for them.
The recommendations should feel achievable.
Never overwhelming.

What Not To Say
This section quietly prevents common communication mistakes.
Each card follows the same format.

Instead Of
"You can't eat that anymore."

Try
"Would you like to look at some options together?"

Another example
Instead Of
"You should exercise more."
Try
"Want to go for a walk together?"

Another
Instead Of
"It's not that serious."
Try
"I know this is a lot to take in."

The goal is not perfect wording.
The goal is empathy.

Everyday Situations
Caregivers often worry about ordinary moments.
This section provides quick guidance.
Examples
Grocery Shopping
Should I stop buying snacks?
↓
Answer
Not necessarily.
It's usually more helpful to plan balanced meals together than to remove every treat from the house.

Family Dinner
Should everyone eat differently now?
↓
Answer
Healthy meals often benefit everyone.
Separate "diabetes food" usually isn't necessary.

Restaurant
What if they order dessert?
↓
Answer
Avoid judging food choices.
One meal does not define diabetes management.

Bad Day
What if they're frustrated?
↓
Answer
Sometimes listening is more helpful than trying to solve the problem.

Each situation is intentionally short.
Users should find answers in under one minute.

Questions To Ask Together
Rather than telling caregivers what to do,
the app encourages conversations.
Examples
What's been the most confusing part so far?
Is there something I could do that would make this easier?
Would you like me to learn about diabetes with you?
Do you want reminders, or would that feel stressful?
How can I support you without taking over?
These questions strengthen communication instead of creating assumptions.

Weekly Caregiver Story
Every week includes one short story from another caregiver.
Example
"My husband was diagnosed last year.
At first I thought I needed to control everything he ate.
Eventually I realized the best thing I could do was simply support the changes he wanted to make."
The stories should be honest.
Not inspirational.
The caregiver should think,
"I'm not the only one figuring this out."

The Shared Journey
One subtle feature connects both experiences.
When the patient completes a major milestone,
the caregiver receives a message.
Example
This week they learned
how blood sugar works.

Maybe ask them to explain it.

Teaching someone else
helps people remember.
This encourages conversation without forcing interaction.
It also reinforces the patient's confidence.

Boundaries
The Caregiver Companion should never encourage controlling behavior.
It should never imply that caregivers are responsible for outcomes.
The app should explicitly discourage:
Food policing.
Medication pressure.
Judgment.
Shame.
Constant reminders.
Monitoring every decision.
Health Decoded supports autonomy.
The patient remains in control of their own care.

Visual Language
The caregiver experience should feel familiar but distinct.
Instead of medical illustrations,
use everyday moments.
Examples:
A couple preparing dinner.
A parent and adult child talking.
Friends walking outside.
Someone listening.
Someone grocery shopping.
The visuals reinforce partnership rather than illness.

Motion Design
Motion should remain subtle.
Cards gently appear.
Conversation examples slide horizontally.
Stories fade in.
Nothing should compete with the written message.
This section should feel calm and reassuring.

Success Criteria
The Caregiver Companion succeeds when caregivers leave feeling more confident and less afraid of saying or doing the wrong thing.
A successful experience changes the caregiver's mindset.
Instead of thinking:
"I need to manage their diabetes."
They begin thinking:
"I can support them while they manage their diabetes."
Likewise, the patient should feel more understood, not more monitored.
The strongest outcome isn't that caregivers know more medical facts.
It's that conversations become calmer, more supportive, and less driven by fear.

Engineering Notes
The Caregiver Companion should be built using the same content engine as the patient journey, allowing each patient lesson to optionally include a corresponding caregiver lesson. This ensures both experiences stay synchronized without duplicating logic. Content should be modular so clinicians can update caregiver guidance independently from patient education. The UI should reuse existing card components, typography, and navigation patterns while using its own illustrations and messaging to create a distinct emotional experience.

Design Principle
If I could summarize this entire feature in one sentence, it would be:
The Caregiver Companion should teach people how to stand beside someone with diabetes, not stand over them.
That's what makes this feature valuable. Most health apps educate the patient and forget everyone else. Health Decoded recognizes that the first 90 days are often a shared experience, and by educating both people, it can improve not only understanding but also the quality of the conversations happening at home.
